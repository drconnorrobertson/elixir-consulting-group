Consulting vs. Coaching: What Your Business Actually Needs

By Dr. Connor Robertson | 2026-04-16

One of the most common questions I get from business owners considering working with Elixir Consulting Group is some version of "do I need a consultant or a coach?" It is a fair question, and the answer matters because hiring the wrong type of support wastes money and, more importantly, time. Here is how I think about it.

What Business Coaching Does

Business coaching is focused on the owner. A coach helps you think through challenges, develop leadership skills, and stay accountable to your goals. Good coaches ask great questions. They help you see blind spots. They push you to follow through on commitments. The best coaching relationships create clarity in the owner's thinking, which leads to better decision-making across the business.

Coaching works well when the owner knows what needs to happen but struggles with execution, prioritization, or confidence. It also works well for owners who are transitioning from operator to leader and need support navigating that identity shift.

What Business Consulting Does

Consulting is focused on the business. A consultant brings expertise, frameworks, and implementation support to solve specific problems or build specific systems. At Elixir Consulting Group, our consulting work typically involves diagnosing operational bottlenecks, designing new processes, building sales systems, implementing AI tools, or restructuring teams for growth.

Consulting works well when you know something is broken but do not have the expertise or bandwidth to fix it internally. You do not need someone to ask you good questions. You need someone to come in, diagnose the problem, build the solution, and help your team adopt it.

The Gray Area

In practice, the best engagements blend both. When I work with a client, I am consulting on their operations and systems, but I am also coaching the owner through the changes. Installing a new operating cadence in a business requires more than a process document. It requires the owner to change how they spend their time, how they delegate, and how they hold people accountable. That is coaching work embedded inside a consulting engagement.

The businesses that get the best results are the ones where the owner is willing to change their own behavior, not just their team's behavior. If you hire a consultant to fix your operations but you continue to override the systems they build, nothing improves. If you hire a coach to help you think bigger but nobody in your business builds the infrastructure to support the vision, nothing changes either.

How to Decide What You Need

Ask yourself two questions. First, do I know what the problem is and how to fix it? If the answer is yes but you are struggling to execute, coaching might be the right starting point. If the answer is no, or if the problem is structural and requires expertise you do not have, consulting is the better path.

Second, is the constraint me or the business? If the biggest bottleneck is your own habits, mindset, or time management, coaching addresses that directly. If the constraint is operational, like broken processes, unclear roles, or missing systems, consulting addresses that directly.

Why We Blend Both at Elixir

At Elixir Consulting Group, I built our engagement model to combine consulting and coaching because I saw too many businesses get one without the other. A client might get brilliant strategic advice from a consultant, but the owner did not have the support to implement it. Or an owner might grow personally through coaching but their business stayed flat because nobody rebuilt the operations.

Our approach is to work on the business and the owner simultaneously. We install systems, build structure, and coach the leadership team through adoption. That is what produces durable results.
