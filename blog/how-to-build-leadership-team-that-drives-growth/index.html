How to Build a Leadership Team That Actually Drives Growth

By Dr. Connor Robertson | 2026-04-18

Most business owners I work with at Elixir Consulting Group are talented operators. They know their product. They know their market. But when I ask them about their leadership team, the answer is often some version of "I am the leadership team." That is the bottleneck that keeps businesses stuck between $1M and $5M in revenue. To break through, you need a leadership team that can execute without you driving every decision.

The Owner Bottleneck

Here is what the owner bottleneck looks like in practice. Every major decision goes through you. Your phone is the default escalation point for every problem. You are the only person who knows the full picture of where the company stands. Your team is talented, but they defer to you on anything that feels uncertain. Sound familiar?

This is not a people problem. It is a structure problem. Your team defers to you because you have not installed the systems that give them the authority, information, and accountability to make decisions on their own. That is fixable.

The Three Pillars of a High-Performing Leadership Team

1. Clear Roles and Decision Rights

Every leader on your team needs to know exactly what they own and what decisions they can make without asking you. This sounds basic, but in most businesses I consult with, role boundaries are fuzzy and decision rights are undefined. I spend the first week of any engagement mapping out who owns what and where the gaps are. The clarity alone often produces immediate relief for the owner and the team.

2. A Weekly Operating Cadence

High-performing leadership teams meet weekly. Not for status updates. For execution accountability. At Elixir Consulting Group, we help clients install a weekly leadership cadence that includes reviewing key metrics, identifying issues, and making decisions. This replaces the constant Slack messages, hallway conversations, and ad-hoc meetings that consume most of your week. When leaders know they have a structured forum to raise issues and get decisions made, they stop interrupting each other (and you) throughout the week.

3. Shared Visibility Into the Numbers

Your leadership team cannot drive growth if they cannot see the dashboard. Every leader should have access to the key metrics that matter for the business: revenue, pipeline, operational throughput, customer satisfaction, and cash position. Too many owners keep the numbers close to the vest. When you share them, you create shared accountability. Leaders start thinking like owners because they can see the impact of their decisions.

Hiring vs. Developing Leaders

One question I get constantly is whether to hire leaders externally or develop them from within. The answer depends on your timeline and the complexity of the role. For most small businesses, developing internal leaders is more effective because they already understand your culture, your customers, and your operations. What they usually lack is training in management, decision-making, and strategic thinking. That is coachable.

External hires make sense when you need a capability that does not exist in your organization today, like a VP of Sales who has built a sales team before, or a head of operations with deep process engineering experience. But external hires fail at a high rate in small businesses because the culture fit is harder to evaluate, and the onboarding investment is significant.

The Framework We Use at Elixir

When I work with a client to build their leadership team, we follow a four-step process. First, we map the current organizational structure and identify where the owner is the bottleneck. Second, we define the leadership seats that need to be filled, either by current team members or new hires. Third, we install the operating cadence and scorecard system so leaders have the structure to execute. Fourth, we coach the leaders through the first 90 days of running in the new structure.

The result is an owner who can step back from day-to-day execution and focus on strategy, growth, and the work that only they can do. That is the unlock that takes businesses from stuck to scaling.
